Rules

  1. Day trading is not a strategy to get rich quickly.
  2. Day trading is not easy. It is serious business, and you should treat is as such.
  3. Day traders do not hold positions overnight. If necessary, you must sell with a loss to make sure you do not hold onto any stock overnight.
  4. Always ask, "Is this stock moving because of the overall market is moving, or is this moving because it has a unique fundamental catalyst?"
  5. Success in day trading comes from risk management - finding low-risk entries with a high potential reward. The minimum win:lose ratio should be 2:1.
  6. Your broker will buy and sell stocks for you at the Exchange. Your only job as a day trader is to manage risk. You cannot be a successful day trader without excellent risk management skills, even if you are the master of many effective strategies.
  7. Retail traders trade only Stocks in Play, high relative volume stocks that have fundamental catalysts and are being traded regardless of the overall market.
  8. Experienced traders are like guerrilla traders. They jump out at just the right time, take their profit, and get out.
  9. Hallow candlesticks, where the close is greater than the open, indicate buying pressure. Filled candlesticks, where the close is less than the open, indicate selling pressure.

The Basics of Day Trading

Day Trading vs Swing Trading

Day trading is the trading of stocks within the same day while swing trading is the trading of stocks over multiple days. Day traders always close their position before the market closes (refer Rule #3).

Long vs Short

When a trader buys stock in the hope that its value will increase is called buying long. Going long entails the actual purchase of the stock and selling it at a higher price, so if someone says they are long on a stock then they intend the price or value to increase. The profit comes from the difference in price from the original purchase value to the next sell value.

The borrowing of stock to with the hope that the price or value will decrease is called selling short. They this works is a trader borrows stock from a broker and sells it right away and then purchases back the stock at a lower price. The broker just wants the shares back and does not want your money, so the profit for the trader is the difference between the original selling price and price that stock is purchases back at.

Retail vs Institution

Individuals like us are called retail traders while investment banks, firms, mutual funds, hedge funds, and other organizations are called institutional traders. Most algorithmic or automated trading is done by institutional traders.

High Frequency Trading (HFT)

As mentioned above, this is the automated trading done by institutional traders using computer algorithms at high speeds. It is important to be mindful and respectful of these stocks current in play by HFT in order to avoid competition and losses.

Trade the best, leave the rest

It is important to play the best stocks that have a fundamental reason to move and are not moving with the market (refer Rule #4). Stocks in play usually have some positive or negative unexpected news like the following:

  • Earnings reports
  • Earning warning or pre-announcements
  • Earnings surprises
  • FDA approvals or disapprovals
  • Mergers or acquisitions
  • Alliances, partnerships, or major product releases
  • Major contract wins or losses
  • Restructurings, layoffs, or management changes
  • Stock splits, buybacks, or debt offerings

Business Management

The following three skills are important to proper business management and its success:

  1. Learning and mastering one or a few proven trading strategies.
  2. Practicing proper risk management.
  3. Controlling your emotions and having sound psychology.

It is recommended to practice the five facets of training outlined by Nike: movement, mindfulness, recovery, nutrition, and sleep. For trading, Andrew recommends a similar five facets: technical knowledge, risk management, nutrition, sound psychology, and sleep.

To minimize risk it is important to ask yourself these questions:

  1. Am I trading the right stock?
    You should avoid stocks that are: heavily traded by computers, have small relative trading volume, penny stocks, and don't have a reason to move (no fundamental catalyst).
  2. What share size should I take?
    This should depend on your account size and daily target earnings. What is your daily trading goal or annual target salary?
  3. What is my stop loss?
    An easy way of looking at this is you should never lose more than 2% of your account in any trade, so if your account is $50,000 then you should not lose more than $1,000, e.g. \($50,000 * 0.02 = $1,000\).

Three-step Risk Management

"It's all about minimizing risk."
  1. Determine your maximum dollar risk for the trade, see #3 above, which should be %2. Calculate this before your trading starts.
  2. Estimate your maximum risk per share, the stop loss, in dollars, from your entry.
  3. Now divide step #1 by step #2 to find the maximum number of shares you are allowed to trade.

For example, if your account has $40,000, the %2 rule will limit your risk to $800 per trade (step #1 above). You see a stock in play and decide to sell short at $50 and you want to cover at $48.82, with a stop loss at $50.40. You will be risking $0.40 per share (step #2 above). For step #3 above, you calculate your share size by dividing \($800.00 / $0.42 = 2000\). Of course you do not have enough money in your account to buy 2000 shares at $50 because \(2000 * $50 = $100,000\), so you buy less like 800 shares which is \(800 * $50 = $40,000\). The point is to make sure that you never risk more than 2% of your account under any circumstance.

Trading Psychology

"Check your emotions at the door."

The most important part is to leave emotions and money out of the picture. To be successful, you must stick to strategy and execution of the strategy like a robot. Fine-tuning of strategies and their execution should take precedence and fit you personally.

It is ok to take a loss for the day and not have it ruin your day. It is not about you as a person but more about having the right strategy and executing it well. This is why it is important to practice and find a strategy that fits your personality well, so play into your strengths and work on your weaknesses.

As mentioned, you should review your strategies over long periods of time to allow for tuning of it for the best outcome. That is why it is important to keep a journal of all your trades, for reflection, and to connect with a community to help.

Event experienced traders can expect to lose 30% of the time and you can be profitable with a risk of 2% and losing 40%, so remember its a numbers and strategy game.

Finding Stocks for Trades

"You are only as good as the stocks you trade."

Stocks in play must move and have enough volume in order to make a profit and minimize risk. This doesn't mean any stock moving but one that is moving in a predictable pattern.

Stocks in Play

Andrew outlines that the best stocks in play have the following characteristics:

  • A stock with fresh news.
  • A stock that is up or down more than 2% before the market open.
  • A stock that has unusual pre-market trading activity.
  • A stock that develops important intraday levels which we can trade off from.

It is recommended that stocks with less than an average daily volume below 500,000 should not be traded because it may be difficult to get out of a stock easily and will cause the trader to be stuck with the stock, therefore increasing the risk and account loss.

Pre-market Gappers

A morning scanner with the following criteria that is the primary list for picking for stocks in play:

  • Stocks that in the pre-market gapped up or down at least 3%.
  • Stocks that have traded at least 50,000 shares in the pre-market.
  • Stocks that have an average daily volume of over 500,000 shares.
  • Stocks that have Average True Range (ATR) of at least $0.50 (how large of a range in price a stock has on average every day).
  • There is a fundamental catalyst for the stock.
  • As a rule, do not trade stocks with an enormous short interest higher than 30% (the short interest is the quantity of stock shares that investors or traders have sold short but not yet covered or closed out).

Real Time Intraday Scans

For some strategies you cannot find socks in the pre-market: The Momentum, Top Reversal, and Bottom Reversal. These are when trading happens during trading hours.

Real Time Volume Radar

  1. Have gapped up or down at least $1.
  2. Have ATR of more than $0.50.
  3. Have average relative volume of at least 1.5 (the stock is trading at 1.5 times its normal volume).
  4. Have average daily volume of at least 500,000 shares.

If there are several stocks that match this criteria in the same sector then they are probably not in play. It is possible that they are being heavily traded by institutional traders.

Real Time Bull Flag Momentum Scanner

For the Momentum Strategy, you need low float stocks that are moving and you cannot find these stocks without a good scanner. These stocks have high relative volume, low float, and high activity.

Real Time Reversal Scanners

Top and Bottom Reversal Strategies are strategies you cannot find stocks for in the pre-market. These are stocks that are selling off or surging up so Reversal strategies can be used.

Planning the Trade Based on Scanners

Select two or three stocks to play and monitor their charts. When a strategy reveals itself, play it (refer Rule #8)! HFT are designed to "shake out" retail traders, so must be smart.

Pattern Day Trade Rule

The U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) and the Financial Industry Regulatory Authority (FINRA) enforce laws that limit the number of trades a trader can make if they are undercapitalized. A Pattern Day Trader (PDT) is someone that buys and sells four or more times in five business days. Under this rule, a pattern day trader must maintain a brokerage account balance of at least $25,000 any day they trade. This is to protect new traders.

This rule can be bypassed by using an offshore trader such as Capital Markets Elite Group Limited (based in Trinidad and Tobago) or Alliance Trader (based in Jamaica). These brokers offer a slightly higher commission fee structure.

Conventional vs Direct-Access

Conventional Brokers aka Full-Service Brokers provide a greater number of services, slower trader execution times, and have higher fees that are better suited for swing trading. Direct-Access Brokers provide faster trade execution at lower fees and are a must for day trading.

NASDAQ Level 2 and Bid-Ask

Level 2 data provides the leading indicator for information before trades happen while moving averages and charts provide lagging indicators. The real time Level 2 data is a must for day trading in order to know who is trading and price action.

A bid is the price a trader is willing to purchase stock for and the ask is the price a trader is willing to sell a stock for. The size is the number of lots of shares and a lot is usually 100 shares, so a size of 2 is 200 shares. A spread is the difference between the bid and ask price. Spreads are usually greater in lower volume stocks.

Buy and Sell Orders

The following are the three important types of orders you can use for day trading:

  1. Market orders: Buy or sell at any price, current market ask-bid price, but do it immediately.
  2. Limit orders: Buy or sell at my set limit price and don't go higher or lower. This is commonly used in swing trading.
  3. Marketable limit orders: Buy or sell at the current market (ask-bid) but do not go higher or lower. This should be used for day trading.

Introduction to Candlesticks

Candlesticks represent the price action and originated in Japan in the 17th century. In order to chart a candlestick you must have the following data: opening price, highest price, lowest price, and closing price all for the desired time frame (daily, 1-minute, 5-minute, etc.). The hollow (white) or filled (red) portion of the candlestick is called "the body". The long thin lines above and below are called "shadows" and represent the high/low range, these are also referred to as "wicks" or "tails". For the time period, if a stock closes higher than its opening price the body will be hallow (white), and if the stock closes at a lower price than its opening it will be filled (red). When the body is hallow (white) the top of the body will be the close price and the bottom will be the open price, and when the body if filled (red) the top of it will be the open and the bottom will be the close. Hallow bodies are bullish and filled bodies are bearish (refer Rule #9).

Price Action and Mass Psychology

At every moment in the market there are basically three categories of traders: the buyers, the sellers, and the undecided. Buyers are looking for deals and want to spend as little as possible, and sellers are wanting to sell their shares for as high as possible. The difference between the two is referred to as the bid-ask spread. The undecided traders are the key in pushing the price higher or lower, and they are feared by all other traders. When buyers keep buying because they think the price will go up the common phrase used is, "Buyers are in control." When the sellers keep selling because they think the price will continue to decrease the phrase, "Sellers are in control", is used. It is fear of the unknown that causes buyers and sellers to buy for future profit or to sell to keep a higher price.

Bullish Candlestick

These candlesticks are large hallow and show that the buyers are in control and tells that the price has potential to go higher.

Bearish Candlestick

These candlesticks are large filled and show that the sellers are in control and tells that the price has potential to go lower.

Indecision Candlesticks

Spinning Tops

These candlesticks have similarly-sized wicks that are usually larger than the body. They represent that the pressure is equal for the buyers and sellers. It is common for the price to change immediately after these.

Doji: Simple, Shooting Star, Hammer

These candlesticks commonly take on the form of a small or nil body. The simple doji means nobody has one the battle yet (equal wicks). The shooting star doji means that the buyers tried to push the price up but did not manage to hold it (the top wick is longer). The hammer doji means the sellers tried to push the price down but did not manage to hold it (the bottom wick is longer).

An example of reversal is when the market is bearish and there have been several filled candlesticks pushing the price down. Then a hammer doji appears shows that the sell off of the stock can no longer be maintained. This is then followed by several hallow candlesticks showing the price going up or recovering from the short. It is important to remember that while this seems clear and easy to trade on all dojis it does not actually represent a market reversal and further indication should be referenced before trading.

Important Day Trading Strategies

This section will focus on strategies based on three elements: price action, technical indicators, and candlestick and chart patterns. As a day trader, you should not be concerned with the details of a company, only the technical analysis of the stock.

Trade Management and Position Sizing

Most novice traders make the mistake and setting up their trades and letting them reach their stop or limit, while more experienced traders will manage their open positions. This is essentially trade management and allows experienced traders to more constantly turn a profit even if two of them are taking opposite positions, long vs short.

Position sizing refers to how large of a position to take during a trade. Load the boat means taking a huge position on an obvious trade. Some other terminology is large for a large position and taste for a small position. It is important to remember risk management and the amount of risk to take on each trade (like 2% loss).

Never average down!!! Take your losses and find another stock in play to trade.

ABCD

  • A - Aggressive buying has pushed the stock to constant new highs for the day.
  • B - Traders who bought the stock earlier start slowly selling it for a profit and the price comes down.
  • C - When a support has been reached and does not go lower and then starts to trend upwards.
  • D - The price as surpassed the previous high at point A and will continue upwards.

The stop loss should be set at point C but trail it upwards to ensure the most profit is taken. After the next surge up at point D the stock should be sold. This pattern gets it name because it resembles a flag pole and waving flag. The pole consists of several large candlesticks going up followed by a series of small candlesticks going sideways.
